The Caesar Cipher

History

The Caesar Cipher was named after Julius Caesar, the dictator who named himself ruler of the Roman Empire. It is one of the earliest ciphers and was used by Caesar to send encrypted messages to his military affiliates.

It was one of the simpler systems used by Caesar, influenced by earlier similar ciphers and later influencing an adapted version adopted by his nephew.

Although it is one of the easiest ciphers to crack, with only 25 possible shifts (each shift corresponding to X shifts along the alphabet), it was relatively effective during Caesar's time. This was largely due to low literacy rates and a lack of knowledge about cryptographic techniques.

However, anyone familiar with the Latin alphabet or the frequency of letters in the alphabet and in the encrypted texts could decipher it successfully. It was therefore not an entirely robust cryptographic method, but one that would discourage casual interception of messages.

The Caesar Cipher has influenced later ciphers, which are inevitably more complex and less vulnerable to translation. Of particular note is the Vigenère cipher, where the shift is determined based on a keyword rather than a fixed shift - known as polyalphabetic substitution.

Despite advancements in ciphers, the Caesar Cipher remains historically important, both as an educational tool for introducing cryptographic techniques and for highlighting the need for cryptography, which has spanned multiple millennia.

How it works

Initially, each character of the alphabet is converted into a number ranging from 0-25 (A=0, B=1, C=2 …) In order to encrypt the message, you would then need the shift key (an amount you'd like to shift the alphabet by..)
For example:

ABC > Shift 1 = BCD

You can also use a minus number ..

ABC > Shift -1 = ZAB
